Random Walk (Drunkard's Stroll)


\[ \begin{equation} \nonumber X_i = \left\{ \begin{array}{l l} \sqrt{\delta} & \quad \textrm{with probability }0.5 \\ -\sqrt{\delta} & \quad \textrm{with probability }0.5 \end{array} \right. \end{equation} \]


\[ \begin{align*} E[X_i] =0, \\ \textrm{Var}(X_i)=\delta. \end{align*} \]

Markov Property

  • position at time \(n + 1\) depends only on your position at time \(n\), not on the path you took to get there.

Markov Chain - Another Example

Brownian Motion / Wiener Process

\[ \begin{align*} W(t)=W(n \delta)=\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i. \end{align*} \]

\[ \begin{align*} E[W(t)]=\sum_{i=1}^{n} E[X_i] =0, \end{align*} \]

\[ \begin{align*} \textrm{Var}(W(t)) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \textrm{Var}(X_i) = n \textrm{Var}(X_1) = n \delta = t. \end{align*} \]

For any \(t \in (0,\infty)\), as \(n\) goes to \(\infty\), \(\delta\) goes to 0. By the central limit theorum, \(W(t)\) will become a normal random variable.

\[ \begin{align*} W(t) \sim N(0, t) \end{align*} \]

Brownian Motion and Finance

  • Could we think of each stock price as a particle moving through a sea of unseen smaller atoms or molecules?
  • Are there any problems with this idea? (QUESTION THE ASSUMPTIONS!)

Geometric Brownian Motion

The prices follow a log-normal distribution, hence:

\[ \begin{align*} log(Y(t))= W(t), \textrm{ where } W(t) \sim N(0, t) \end{align*} \]

Which implies that

\[ \begin{align*} Y(t)= e^{W(t)}, \textrm{ where } W(t) \sim N(0, t) \end{align*} \]

A stock price usually has some sort of trend, so lets add what is known as a "drift":

\[ \begin{align*} Y(t)= e^{W(t) + \mu t}, \textrm{ where } W(t) \sim N(0, t) \end{align*} \]

Hence we can model a stock return as follows:

\[ \begin{align*} S(t) = S_0 e^{Y(t)}, \textrm{ where } Y(t) = \sigma W(t) + \mu t \end{align*} \]
